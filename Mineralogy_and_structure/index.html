Consistency of fine-grained soils

Atterberg limits are used to describe the behavior (specifically plasticity) of the fine fraction in the soil.
  • If you keep adding water to the soil, it will eventually behave as a liquid
  • Water content is important, but not all soils behave the same at same water content.
  • We need to map the water content to soil behavior and plasticity

Plasticity

Is the degree of a soil to be molded without noticeable elastic rebound or volume change and without cracking or crumbling.
We use plasticity to:
  1. Differentiate between plastic (e.g., clay) and non-plastic (e.g., silt) fines.
  2. Further classify soils by degree of plasticity.

Consistency indices

\(PI\): Is the range of water content in which the fine fraction of soils behaves as a plastic material.
\( \begin{align} PI=LL-PL \end{align} \)
\(LI\): is a scaling parameter that maps the natural water content to the consistency limits. it indicates how close a soil's natural water content is to its liquid limit, relative to its plasticity index
\( \begin{align} LI=\cfrac{w_n-PL}{PI} \end{align} \)

Where \(w_n\) is the natural water content of the soil.

Liquid limit (ASTM D4318)

\(LL\): Is the water content at which a standard groove cut in the soil will close 0.5" at 25 blows.
  • Arthur Casagrande refined the method to measure \(LL\) so that is operator independent.
  • This method is known as the Casagrande cup method.

Casagrande's LL determination procedure

  1. Take only dry soil passing sieve 40. These are fine sands + fines.
  2. Rehydrate the soil and put it into the brass cup.
  3. Carve a grove through the soil paste using a standard grooving tool.
  4. Turn handle at 2 rpm to close gap.
  5. Stop when contact length is 13 mm
  6. Determine water content at this point
  7. Repat for different water contents so that the number of blows range from 10 to 40.

Plastic limit (ASTM D4318)

\(PL\): Is the water content at which soil crumbles when rolled to a diameter of 3 mm.
  • Determined by rolling clay snakes
  • Results are operator dependent.
  • Repeat at least three times.
  • \(PL\) is the average water content of the total number of tests performed.
Notes on Atterberg Limits
  • Typical range of \(LL\) can be from 0 to 1,000, but most soils have \(LL < 100\)%.
  • The \(PL\) can range from 0 to more than 100, with most soils having \(PL < 40\)%.
  • These tests are performed in remolded soil. This is well-mixed/blended; not the same state as in the ground.
  • These tests are very arbitrary and simple
  • Why do they work to categorize soil behavior?
    Like many things in geotechnical engineering, they work empirically. That is, they correlate with engineering properties because both are affected by the same things.

Soil classification

Geotechnical Engineers classify soils using the Unified Soil Classification System (USCS) for consistent naming.

Important concepts for USCS soil classification:

  1. Coarse-grained soils are classified by grain size distribution.
  2. Fine-grained soils are classified by engineering behavior e.g., plasticity.
  3. Only sieve analysis and Atterberg Limits are used to classify soils

Soil 1:
Step 1: Get the soil fractions:
\( \begin{align} G_F & = 100 -99= 1 \% \\ F_F & = 60 \% \\ S_F & = 100 - 60 -1 = 39 \% \\ \end{align} \)

Is the \(F_F \geq 50\%\)? Answer: Yes \(\implies\) Fine-grained soil (use plast. chart).
From plasticity chart:
Soil is: CL-ML
Is the coarse fraction > 30%? Answer: Yes \(\implies\) We need to check secondary descriptor
Since \(S_F > G_F\) \(\implies\) secondary descriptor is Sandy
Is \(G_F \geq 15\%\)? Answer: No \(\implies\) Final soil classification is CL-ML Sandy silty clay

Soil 2:
Step 1: Get the soil fractions:
\( \begin{align} G_F & = 100 -97= 3 \% \\ F_F & = 5 \% \\ S_F & = 100 - 5 -3 = 92 \% \\ \end{align} \)
Is the \(F_F \geq 50\%\)? Answer: No \(\implies\) Coarse-grained soil (use coarse flowchart).
Since \(S_F > G_F\) \(\implies\) first letter is S
What is \(F_F\)? Answer: \(F_F = 5\%\) \(\implies\) Dual symbols
\(D_{60}=0.8\)mm, \(D_{30}=0.30\) mm, \(D_{10}=0.17\) mm
\(C_u = D_{60}/D_{10} = 0.8/0.17 = 4.7 < 6\)
\(C_c = (D_{30})^2/(D_{60}*D_{10}) = (0.30)^2/(0.8*0.17) = 0.66 < 1\)
Since \(C_u < 6\) and/or \(C_c < 1\) is true \(\implies\) second letter is P
Since soil is non-plastic dual descriptor is M \(\implies\) Soil is: SP-SM
Is \(G_F \geq 15\%\)? Answer: No \(\implies\) Final soil classification is SP-SM Poorly graded sand with silt

Soil 3:
Step 1: Get the soil fractions:
\( \begin{align} G_F & = 100 -100= 0 \% \\ F_F & = 97 \% \\ S_F & = 100 - 97 -0 = 3 \% \\ \end{align} \)
Is the \(F_F \geq 50\%\)? Answer: Yes \(\implies\) Fine-grained soil (use plast. chart).
From plasticity chart:
Use A-line equation: \(PI_{A_{line}}=0.73*(LL-20)=0.73*(124-20)=76\)
Since \(PI > PI_{A_{line}} \implies\) soil is CH
Is the coarse fraction > 30%? Answer: No \(\implies\) No secondary descriptor needed
Soil is: CH Fat clay
