History of soil mechanics

Soil mechanics is a science with a long history.

In 1773, the French physicist and engineer Charles-Augustin de Coulomb published a theory of earth pressure. This theory is still used to calculate lateral forces on retaining walls.

History of soil mechanics

In 1857, the Scotish engineer William Rankine published another theory of earth pressure. Similar to Coulomb's theory, Rankin's work is widely used in soil mechanics practice.

History of soil mechanics

Otto Mohr (circa 1880) improved Coulomb's theory and developed graphical techniques for two-dimensional strain-stress states..

History of soil mechanics

In 1856, French engineer, Henri Philibert Gaspard Darcy studied the permeability of sands. This leds Darcy to postulate Darcy's law, a mathematical expression that relates soil permeability, hydraulic head, and flow velocity of soils.

History of soil mechanics

In 1885, the physicist and mathematician, Joseph Valentin Boussinesq solved the stresses induced by external loads for elastic continuum media.

History of soil mechanics

In 1911, Swedish soil scientist and chemist, Albert Mauritz Atterberg proposed the consistency limits of shrinkage, plasticity, and liquidity. These limits are also known as Atterberg limits.

History of soil mechanics

In 1925, Austrian Geologist and Civil Engineer, Karl von Terzaghi published the most consequential theory of soil mechanics in his book entitled "Erdbaumechanik" (translated: Earthwork of Mechanics). In this work, Terzaghi proposed the effective stress principle, generally accepted as the birth of modern soil mechanics.

Terzaghi is known as the Father of Geotechnical Engineering.

History of soil mechanics

Around 1930, Arthur Casagrande improved the test procedures developed by Atterberg and proposed the Unified Soils Classification System (USCS) used to classify soils according to their grain size distribution and consistency.

Weathering

Soils are formed by the disintegration of rocks. This process is known as weathering and occurs at different rates in nature. Weathering divided into:

PHYSICAL

  • Root wedging.
  • Frost wedging.
  • Salt wedging.
  • Exfoliating.
  • Thermal expansion.

CHEMICAL

  • Hydration.
  • Oxydation.
  • Dissolution.

Transported and residual soils

Soils can be transported or they can stay in place. When they stay in place they are called residual soils.

Agents that transport soils:

  • Water
  • Air
  • Ice
  • Gravity

Note:

Transportation is a fundamental process in the geological cycle. Transported soils can transform into rocks with pressure and temperature.

Soil is a collection of particles of diverse size and shape.

Soils

We can classify them by particle size \(d\) according to the Unified Soil Classification System (USCS- ASTM D2487):

  • Boulders: \(d>\) 300 mm \(\approx\) 1 ft
  • Cobbles: 75 \(< d \leq \) 300 mm \(\approx\) 3 in - 1 ft
  • Gravels: 4.75 \( < d \leq \) 75 mm
  • Sands: 0.075 \( < d \leq \) 4.75 mm
  • Silt: \(2 \times 10^{-6}< d \leq \) 0.075 mm
  • Clay: \(d \leq 2 \times 10^{-6}\) mm

Alluvial deposits

.- These are soils transported and deposited in a riverine environment. Alluvial deposits can be complex due to changes in flow velocity during flood and droughts cycles.
  • Particles are usually rounded.
  • Usually uniform gradation (soil particles have similar size).
  • Tey will often consist of coarse-sized material.

Estuarine deposits

.- These are soils transported and deposited in estuaries. Estuarine deposits are highly complex due to interactions of river flow, tides, and waves.
  • Interlayering or mixtures of sand, silts, clay, and organic matter.
  • Can form deltas.
  • Very soft fine grained deposits.

Beaches

.- These are important coastal features deposited and transported by waves, tides, and wind. Beaches are usually uniform and respond rapidly to changing conditions of flow velocity.
  • Governed by wave currents across and along the shoreline.
  • Sediments migrate very rapidly.
  • Responds quickly to sea level rise.
  • Influenced by on-shore activities.

Lacustrine/marine soils

.- These are two distinct type of deposits. They origin and deposition mechanism are similar. These soils are deposited in near zero velocity flows. If the soil is deposited offshore this is a marine soil, and if it is deposited in lakes it is a lacustrine soil.
  • Usually they are made of fine soils and/or organic matter.
  • The chemistry of clay particles in marine sediments is influenced by salt water.

Glacial deposits

.- These are soils transported by glaciers and deposited with melting processes. These soils are very heterogenous and complex.
  • Variety of mixed particle sizes. From clay to boulders.
  • Made of angular clasts.
  • Old glacial deposits are known as till.

Eolian deposits

.- These are soils transported and deposited by wind such as sand dunes and loess. Eolian deposits can be uniform. In the case of coastal dunes, particles are very rounded. Loess have a weak structure and they are prone to collapse with changes of water content.
  • Common in arid regions and some coastal areas.
  • Sand dunes travel pretty quickly.
  • Loess are made of wind blown silt particles.

Colluvial deposits

.- These are soils transported and deposited by gravity. These are the remainder of landslides and rockfall. Colluvial soils are heterogeneous.
  • Mixed particle size.
  • Angular shape.
  • Indicate a zone of instability.

Residual deposits

.- Forms in place due to chemical processes. These are very complex and heterogeneous with depth.
  • Properties depend of parent rock.
  • Particle size assortment with depth.
  • Difficult to characterize.
