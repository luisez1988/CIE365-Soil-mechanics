Soil mechanics

  • According to Holtz et al (2023):

    "Is the branch of geotechnical engineering. concerned with the engineering mechanics and properties of soils".

  • Soil mechanics integrates kinematics, dynamics, fluid mechanics, and material mechanics to soils. Soil, rather than water, steel, or concrete, becomes the engineering material whose properties and behavior we must understand to build with or upon it (Holtz et al. 2023).

Origin of soils

Soils are formed by the disintegration of rocks. This process is known as weathering and can be divided into:

PHYSICAL

  • Root wedging.
  • Frost wedging.
  • Salt wedging.
  • Exfoliating.
  • Thermal expansion.

CHEMICAL

  • Hydration.
  • Oxydation.
  • Dissolution.

Transported and residual soils

Soils can be transported or they can stay in place. When they stay in place they are called residual soils.

Agents that transport soils:

  • Water
  • Air
  • Ice
  • Gravity

Note:

Transportation is a fundamental process in the geological cycle. Transported soils can transform into rocks with pressure and temperature.

Soils

Alluvial deposits

These are soils transported and deposited in a riverine environment. Alluvial deposits can be complex due to changes in flow velocity during flood and droughts cycles.
  • Particles are usually rounded.
  • Usually uniform gradation (soil particles have similar size).
  • They will often consist of coarse-sized material.

Estuarine deposits

Soils transported and deposited in estuaries. Estuarine deposits are highly complex due to interactions of river flow, tides, and waves.
  • Layering or mixtures of sand, silts, clay, and organic matter.
  • Can form deltas.
  • Very soft fine grained deposits.

Beaches

These are important coastal features deposited and transported by waves, tides, and wind. Beaches are usually uniform and respond rapidly to changing conditions of flow velocity.
  • Governed by wave currents across and along the shoreline.
  • Sediments migrate very rapidly.
  • Responds quickly to sea level rise.

Lacustrine/marine soils

These are two distinct type of deposits. Their origin and deposition mechanism are similar. These soils are deposited in near zero velocity flows. If the soil is deposited offshore it is a marine soil, and if it is deposited in lakes it is a lacustrine soil.
  • Usually they are made of fine soils and/or organic matter.
  • The chemistry of clay particles in marine sediments is influenced by salt water.

Glacial deposits

These are soils transported by glaciers and deposited during thaw processes. These soils are very heterogenous and complex.
  • Variety of mixed particle sizes. From clay to boulders.
  • Made of angular clasts.
  • Old glacial deposits are known as till.

Eolian deposits

They are transported by wind. Examples are: coastal dunes with very rounded particles; and loess. Loess have a weak structure and are prone to wetting collapse.
  • Common in arid regions and some coastal areas.
  • Sand dunes travel pretty quickly.
  • Loess are made of wind blown silt particles.

Colluvial deposits

They are transported and deposited by gravity and are the remainder of landslides and rockfall.
  • Heterogeneous materials.
  • Mixed particle size.
  • Angular shape.
  • Indicate a zone of instability.

Residual deposits

Forms in place due to chemical processes. These are very complex and heterogeneous with depth.
  • Properties depend of parent rock.
  • Particle size assortment with depth.
  • Difficult to characterize.
