The block analogy

The Mohr-Coulomb model

It is a simple linearization of the ultimate strength of soils or a limiting state when soils start to accumulate plastic deformation

$$\tau_f=S=c+\sigma \tan \phi$$

  • \(\tau_f=S=\) the shear strength of the soil
  • \(c=\) cohesion
  • \(\phi=\) friction angle

Friction of soils

Friction is a fundamental phenomena in nature, that its basic level depends on the materials in contact. In soils it is influenced by different factors.

  • Mineralogy
  • Particle size
  • Particle shape
  • Stress state
  • Stress path
  • Soil density
  • OCR
  • Anysotropy

Dilation of soils

Is the capacity of soils to expand its volume when sheared

$$D=\cfrac{d \varepsilon_v} {d \varepsilon_q}$$

  • \(D=\) dilation
  • \(\varepsilon_q =\) deviatoric strain
  • \(\varepsilon_v =\) volumetric strain

Triaxial test

In the triaxial test a cell pressure \(\sigma_3\) is applied and the axial strain isincreases until the sample "fails".

Drainage and consolidation are controlled. We can have:

  1. Unconsolidated-undrained triaxial test (UU)
  2. Consolidated-undrained triaxial test (CU)
  3. Consolidated-drained triaxial test (CD)

Direct shear test

Shear stress is increased until sample fails in a predefined failure plane.

  • Conducted on consolidated and drained conditions.
  • Is \(K_0\)-consolidated \(\longrightarrow\) Real in-situ conditions.
  • Conservative for soils with pre-defined failure planes.
  • Produces rotation of principal stresses

Simple shear test

Closest to pure shear test. Sample is distorted from ends.

  • Conducted on consolidated, constant-volume and drained conditions.
  • \(K_0\)-consolidated test.
  • Produces rotation of principal stresses

    • In-situ stress paths

    Shear strength of sands

    Sand is inhert-uncharged particles that are permeable.

    • Under normal loading conditions, sand behaves in a drained manner.
    • Shear strength is a function of confinement pressure and relative density.
    • In drained conditions, dilatancy causes a peak.
    • At large deformations, samples with identical cell pressure reach the same ultimate strength. This is known as critical state.
    • For drained tests: Dense samples show shear banding and loose samples cause buldging.
    • For undrained tests: Dense samples bulge and loose samples have shear banding.
    • If loads are applied rapiddly on loose sand (e.g., during earthquakes) the sand liquefies.

    Strength parameters of sand

    When using the Mohr-Coulomb model we need to define either drained or undrained parameters. For sand we typically use drained parameters. What are these parameters for sand?

    Recall: $$\tau_f=c'+\sigma'_{nf} \tan \phi'$$

    Sands have \(c'=0\) unless there is cementation. However, different friction angles exist depending on deformation level or definition of failure.

    • \(\phi'_p=\) peak friction angle
    • \(\phi'_s=\) secant friction angle
    • \(\phi'_r=\) repose friction angle
    • \(\phi'_c=\) critical friction angle

    \(K_0\) formula

    Anlaytical and emphirical expressions have been developed for \(K_0\) as a function of the friction angle. These equations are often used to determine \(K_0\) for sand

    \(K_0^{nc}=1-\sin \phi'_c\) \(\longrightarrow\) For NC soil (Jacky 1944)

    \(K_0^{oc}=K_0^{nc} OCR^{0.5}\) \(\longrightarrow\) For OC soil (Meyerhof 1976)

    Strength of clay

    The low permeability and stress-history of clay hughly influence its strength parameters.

    • Under normal loading conditions, clay can take years to dissipate pore pressures. Then it acts as it was undrained
    • In long term, those exces pore pressures will dissipate and then it can be seen as a frained case.
    • The behavior of clays is controlled by \(OCR\).
    • Clay with \(OCR>1\) have tendecies to dilate
    • Normally consolidated clays contract
    • Clay also has a critical state.
    • Mineralogy changes can affect the strength of clay.
    • Sensitivity (effect of remolding or changing the fabric) can has a drastic effect on soil behavior.

    Undrained strength of clay

    At short term conditions, the boundary conditions of the clay are similar to those of the UU test. Because of that we use the undrained shear strength \(S_u=c_u\).

    We use total stresses with \(S_u\). This is a "trick" we use because it is difficult to predict excess pore pressure during shear.

    Drained strength of clay

    In some cases we want to perform drained analysis of clay. In those situations we nee to know the parameters of clay.

    • Recall, normally consolidated clay contracts and overconsolidated clay dilates.
    • For normally consolidated clay \(c'=0\). Then we typically get a unique friction angle.
    • For overconsolidated clay, cohesion is observed. This cohesion is not necesarly due to cementation but due to suction during dilation.
    • As \(OCR\) increases, \(c'\) increases
    • As \(OCR\) increases, \(\phi'\) decreases

    Relationship between \(OCR\) and \(S_u\)

    Te undrained shear strength is highly related to the sample depth and OCR. For normally consolidated clay the folowing relationship is expected:

    $$\cfrac{S_u}{\sigma'_{v0}}=USR$$

    • \(USR=\) undrained strength ratio
    • Mesri (1975) found a typical value of 0.22

    Relationship between \(OCR\) and \(S_u\)

    For overconsolidated soils the equation is transformed into:

    $$\left(\cfrac{S_u}{\sigma'_{v0}}\right)_{oc}=USR_{oc}=USR_{nc} OCR^{k}$$

    • \(k\) is typically obtained using the SHAMSEP procedure.
    • \(k=0.8\) for Jamiolkowski et al. (1985)
