Total head

Bernoulli's equation describes the total energy of an incompressible flow in terms of its potential, kinetic, and pressure energy.

\(E=z \rho g + \rho \cfrac{v^2}{2g} + P \)

\(h\): Is the total energy per unit weight of water.
\(h=E/\gamma_w\)
  • \(\rho=\) density
  • \(g=\) gravity
  • \(z=\) Elevation
  • \(v=\) velocity
  • \(P=\) pressure
Note: Within soils the kinetic energy is negligible.

Total head

\(h\): Is the total energy per unit weight of water.
\(h=z+\cfrac{u}{\gamma_w}\)
\(h_w\): Is the height of the water column from the point of interest to the zero water pressure line or piezometric line.
\(h_w=\cfrac{u}{\gamma_w}\)

Note: The piezometric line is not necessarily the same as the water table.

Water in movement

Water flows if and only if there is a total head gradient (i.e., difference in total head \(\Delta h \neq 0 \) ). If the flow moves from point A to B, then \(h_a>h_b\)

Hydraulic gradient

\(i\): Is the gradient of total head, or change of total head (\(\Delta h\)) over the length of flow path ( \(L\)).
\(i=\cfrac{\Delta h}{L}=\cfrac{h_a-h_b}{L_{ab}} \)

Although the hydraulic gradient could be negative we usually calculate it using \(h_a>h_b\)

  • \(i=0\) \(\longrightarrow\) no flow.
  • \(i \neq 0\) \(\longrightarrow\) flow between the two points.

Darcy's law

Was proposed by Henry Darcy (1803-1858) after measurements of flow and hydraulic heads.

He observed that the flow rate \(q\) was:

  • Proportional to the cross sectional area \(A\) and the head difference \(\Delta h\).
  • Inversely proportional to \(L\).
\(q=k \frac{\Delta h}{L} A= kiA\)

\(k=\) Coefficient of permeability

\(k\) [L/T]: Is a property of the soil related to how fast water can seep through the soil matrix. The permeability is tightly related to the soil fabric and pore network distribution.

Darcy's law is valid if:

  • The water flow is slow, laminar, not turbulent.
  • Kinematic energy is negligible.
  • The soil is homogeneous (i.e., properties do not depend on the location of measurement).
  • The soil is isotropic (i.e., properties do not depend on the direction that you measure them).

Measurement of permeability

  • Is the parameter that measures ability of water to flow trough soil.
  • Depends on void ratio, GSD, voids connectivity, viscosity of the fluid,...
  • One of the most variable parameters of soils and difficult to determine in the field accurately.
  • Different methods and between most popular we have:
    1. Constant head test
    2. Falling head.

Constant head permeability test

Assuming \(\Delta h=\)cnt. the permeability is calculated measuring the total volume of water collected (\(Q\)) in a time period (\(\Delta t\))

\(k=\cfrac{QL}{A \Delta h \Delta t}\)

  • Better for coarse soils.
  • Shorter testing times.
  • Samples with large cross-sectional areas.

Falling head permeability test

Permeability is calculated measuring the head fall \(\Delta H\) in time \(\Delta t\). The head is not constant.

\(k=\cfrac{a}{A}\cfrac{L}{\Delta t} \ln \left(\cfrac{H_1}{H_1-\Delta H}\right)\)

  • Better suited for low permeability soils
  • Longer testing times
  • Soil samples with smaller cross-sectional areas.

Typical permeability of soils

Rule of thumb:

Soil type Permeability \(k\) [cm/s]
Gravel \(> 10^{-1}\)
Sand \(10^{-3}\) to \(10^{-1}\)
Silt \(10^{-5}\) to \(10^{-3}\)
Clay \(< 10^{-5}\)

Empirical correlations

  • Hazen's (1911) equation:

    \(k[cm/s]=C D_{10}^2\)

    • \(D_{10}\) in mm
    • \(C=\) empirical coefficient between 0.4-10.0. 1 is a good value for clean poorly graded sands.
  • Chapuis's formula

    \(k[cm/s]=2.4622\left(D_{10}^2 \cfrac{e^3}{1+e}\right)\)

    • \(D_{10}\) in mm
  • Kozeny and Carman's formula:

    \(k[cm/s]=\cfrac{\gamma_w}{\eta_w}\cfrac{1}{C_{k-c}S^2_s}\cfrac{e^3}{1+e}\)

    • \(\gamma_w\) in kN/m3
    • \(\eta_w=\) viscosity of water \(1.002\times10^{-3}\) Ns/m2
    • \(C_{k-c}=\) Kozeny-Carman's empirical constant (2 is usually used).
    • \(S_s=\) specific surface 1/cm

Equivalent permeability

If the soil is conformed of layers with different permeability, we need to calculate an equivalent permeability in order to apply Darcy's law.

We will study two cases:

  1. Flow is perpendicular to the layering.
  2. Flow is parallel to the layering.
