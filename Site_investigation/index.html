What is a key difference between these materials?

Concrete and steel are man-made materials. Soils are natural materials. Therefore, We must investigate and characterize them first!

Site investigation

Objectives
  • Stratigraphy
  • Soil properties
  • Establish groundwater conditions
  • Hazards
Purpose
  • Assess the site's suitability.
  • Assess existing geohazards in the site.
  • Achieve cost-effective, resilient, and safe design.
  • Identify issues that may arise during execution.

Desk study

Purpose: Find information on the following:
  • Probable ground conditions (e.g., topography, soil and rock type, groundwater table)
  • Previous land-use
  • Access to the site
Why do desk studies?

Cheaper to obtain than to generate new data.

Information typically available:
  • Geological maps
  • Aerial photographs
  • Topographic maps
  • Previous site investigation reports

Case history 3.1: The Oso Landslide

Draw conclusions on the suitability of land development in the zone marked in red. What is important to look at in this aerial image?
  • Precedent is important: Comparing aerial images in time, tells you history of the site. Visible scars, bumpy terrain, and visible deformations over time indicate instability
  • Always check the water: Water erodes and deposits materials, but also infiltrates in porous media reducing its strength. Understanding how water moves in a site is key
  • High value, zero cost: Many geohazards can be identified from aerial images at global scales. Never skip the view from above

Walk-over surveys

The purpose is to check and make additions to the information already collected during the desk study.
Should include:
  1. Inspection of the site and surrounding area on foot
  2. Examination of the local records not included in the desk study
  3. Surveying local residents about the site

Features that should be identified:

  1. Slopes angles
  2. Exposures
  3. Vegetation
  4. Instabilities (scarps, tension cracks)
  5. Water conditions (surface and subsurface)
  6. Structures
  7. Access

Subsurface exploration

The purpose is to determine:
  1. Soil types
  2. Soil strength
  3. Depth to bedrock
  4. Rock type and quality
  5. Groundwater levels
Three components of exploration:
  1. Boring or excavation
  2. Sampling
  3. Testing:
    1. Laboratory testing
    2. In-situ testing

Boring

Purpose:
  1. Obtain samples of geologic materials for classification and laboratory testing
  2. Enable in-situ measurements of the physical and engineering properties of soil

How many boreholes and what layout should I use for a project?

  1. Number, type, location, and depth of investigation depend on:
    1. Nature and size of project

    3. Budget for site investigation

    5. Depth of investigation depends on extend to which the soil is affected by external loads/factors

Sampling methods

Soil samples are collected at various depths during drilling for further analysis. Depending on objective, two types of samples can be recovered:

  1. Disturbed samples: Used for soil classification and index properties. Obtained when soil structure is altered during sampling.
  2. Intact ("undisturbed") samples: Used for determining engineering properties. Obtained when soil structure is preserved during sampling.

Shelby tubes

  • Thin-walled, hollow steel tubes for geotechnical investigation
  • Typically used in soft to medium silts and clays
  • Pushed into the ground, not driven, to minimize disturbance
  • Use to determine engineering properties in the lab (e.g., strength, compressibility)

In-situ testing

In-situ tests are used to obtain information on the characteristics of soil, when intact sampling is not feasible (e.g., for coarse-grained soils)

Most popular devices are:

  1. Standard Penetration Test (SPT)
  2. Cone Penetrometer Test (CPT)
  3. Vane Shear Test (VST)
  4. Pressuremeter Test (PMT)
  5. Flat Plate Dilatometer Test (DMT)
