What are geostatic stresses?
Is a field of stress generated by the soil's own weight and influenced by intersticial pore water pressure
Recall: Stress is defined as the force per unit area applied on a material.
Since force is a vectorial entity, stress is also a multi-dimensional value

Water in hydrostatic conditions

In hydrostatic conditions, pore water pressure (\(u\) ) is controlled by the ground water table (GWT) elevation.
If the soil is fully dry \(u=0\)
If the soil is partially saturated \(u<0\). Suction is generated by the water surface tension and pore size.
Below the GWT \(u \geq 0\).
Pore pressure is calculated based on depth measured from the water table.


The hydrostatic pore water pressure is then calculated as:
\( u= h_w \gamma_w = h_w g \rho_w \)

Where:
\(h_w\) is the depth below the GWT

Principle of effective stress

Consider a fully saturated soil. At the grain level the sum of pore pressure and inter-particle forces must balance the total stress applied to the soil element.
Thus,
\( \sigma= \sigma' + u \)

This called the principle of effective stress
  • \(\sigma'\) is called the intergranular or effective stress and is the stress that is transmitted through the particle contacts
  • \(\sigma\) is the total stress applied to the soil element and is the sum of the intergranular stress and the pore water pressure.
Thus, soil deformations is caused by changes of effective stress
Note: The principle of effective stresses only applies for dry and fully saturated soils. For partially saturated soils the pressure of the gases entrapped in the soil must be considered.

In-situ total vertical stresses

\(\sigma_v\): Is the sum of all vertical stresses above a depth of interest in the subsurface.

We can calculate this total weight by adding all weights above the depth of interest:

\( \begin{align} \sigma_v= \int_{z=0}^{z=h} \gamma(z) dz \end{align} \)

In a multilayered system (i.e., the typical scenario) you can add the individual weight of each layer above the point of interest.

The total vertical stress at depth \(z\) is:

\( \begin{align} \sigma_v=\sum_{i=1}^n \gamma_i h_i \end{align} \)

\(n=\) number of interfaces below the ground surface including the water table.

Horizontal stresses

Horizontal effective stresses are calculated based on the capacity of the soil to deform laterally. When the soil doesn't deform laterally it is in at-rest conditions.
\(K_0\): Is the ratio of the effective horizontal stress \(\sigma'_h\) to the effective vertical stress \(\sigma'_v\).

\(K_0=\cfrac{\sigma'_h}{\sigma'_v}\)

\(K_0\) is only valid for horizontal surface conditions and only to effective stresses
\(\sigma_h\): The total horizontal stress can be calculated using the effective stress principle.
\(\sigma_h= \sigma'_h+ u\)
