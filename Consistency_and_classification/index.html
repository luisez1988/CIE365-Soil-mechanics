Specific surface

  • Coarse particles have lower \(SSA\) than fine particles.
  • A high \(SSA\) means more chemical and physical reactions. More area is in contact with other chemicals.
  • A high \(SSA\) means more area for drag. Finer soils are less permeable than coarse soils.

Clay mineralogy

Clay particles are created by chemical weathering of rocks. This forms phyllosilicates (hydrous aluminum silicates). Clay particles are composed of two simple blocks:

Tetrahedral silica

Octahedral alumina

Clay particles are charged particles!

Effect of water on clays

Recall water is a dipole particle. As a result, water molecules will interact with the interlayer ions and the particle surface charge.

Consequences:

  • Minerals with larger \(SSA\) are more influenced by water.
  • Added water expands the interlayer space. This soils are known as expansive soils.
  • Expansion is reversible. When water content is reduced the interlayer thickness is reduced and clays shrink.
  • The change of water content produces changes in consistency.

Consistency limits

Also known as Atterberg limits after soil scientist who proposed them (Albert Atterberg). The Atterberg limits are thresholds of water content at which the behavior of soils changes.

There are three important limits:

  1. The liquid limit.
  2. The plastic limit.
  3. The shrinkage limit.

Note:

You will perform tests to determine this limits in your laboratory practice. You will have fun!

Liquid limit (ASTM D4318)

\(LL\): Is the water content at which a standard groove cut in the soil will close 0.5" at 25 blows.
  • Proposed by Arthur Casagrande who designed a method to measure the \(LL\) (a.k.a the Casagrande cup method).
  • Most soils will have \(LL\) less than 100%. However, it can be as high as 300%.

Procedure to determine \(LL\)

  1. Add water to soil sample passing sieve No 40.
  2. Put soil in Casagrande cup.
  3. Use grooving tool to create a groove in soil.
  4. Rotate handle and count blows.
  5. Stop when gap closes approximately 0.5" of length
  6. Sample the soil to determine water content.
  7. Repeat steps at least 4 times.
  8. Plot \(w\) vs. \(N_{blows}\). The \(N\) axis must be in logarithmic scale.
  9. Interpolate \(LL\) from curve.

Plastic limit (ASTM D4318)

\(PL\): Is the water content at which soil crumbles when rolled to a diameter of 3 mm.
  • Results are user dependent.
  • Most soils have \(PL < 40\)%.

Procedure to determine \(PL\)

  1. Spread mixed soil in glass surface to dry it out.
  2. Roll the soil to a 3 mm diameter
  3. If soil crumbles, take moisture sample
  4. Repeat at least three times.
  5. \(PL\) is the average of water contents.

Shrinkage limit (ASTM D4943)

\(SL\): Is the water content at which volume change stops when the soil is further desiccated.
  • Below the PL, water exserts suction.
  • This suction reduces the volume of voids.
  • When soil is desiccated a change of color is observed.

Procedure to calculate \(SL\)

  1. Pour wet soil in container of known volume and weigh it.
  2. Put the sample in the oven until it is fully dry. This will shrink the sample.
  3. Measure change of volume using mercury.
  4. Calculate \(SL\) using:
    \(SL=\left(\frac{V_{dry} \gamma_w}{W_s}-\frac{1}{G_s}\right) \times 100\)

Consistency indices

\(PI\): Is the range of water content in which the fine fraction of soils behaves as a plastic material.
$$ PI=LL-PL $$
\(LI\): is a scaling parameter that maps the natural water content to the consistency limits.
$$ LI=\cfrac{w_n-PL}{PI} $$

Soil classification

Soil classification is process that allows Geotechnical Engineers to assign a name to a soil consistently. Geotechnical Engineers use the Unified Soil Classifcation System (USCS).

Group symbols

  • Two letters
    • First letter \(\longrightarrow\) dominant fraction.
    • Second letter \(\longrightarrow\) minor fraction
  • Dominant fraction
    • Coarse (retained in sieve No 200).
      • Gravel (G), sand (S).
    • Fines (passing sieve No 200).
      • Silt (M), clay (C), organic content (O).
  • Minor fraciton
    • Coarse, depending on well/poorly-graded (W, P) and fines (M, C).
    • Fine, depending on low/high plasticity (L,H).
